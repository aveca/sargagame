Before you start

The satellite sees the mats coming. Use it.

In the French Antilles, sargassum isn't a weather surprise: the mats drift in from the Atlantic over several days, and the satellite spots them well before they reach your waterfront. So a hotel's problem is almost never the information itself — it's having it early enough, at the scale of your beach, and having decided in advance what to do with it.

This guide is an operational roadmap for a hotel manager. Five steps, ideally set up before the season starts (from April), to move from firefighting to piloting. Each one can be put in place in a few days.

The 5-step checklist

From the forecast to the front desk, what to lock in.

Subscribe to the per-beach forecast

This is the foundation. A regional map tells you "risk on the Atlantic coast" — too broad to decide on. What you need is a 7-day satellite forecast for the property's exact waterfront, with a status (clean / moderate / avoid), a score and a trend. Without that visibility ahead of time, every following step stays reactive.

Do this week

Identify the beach (or beaches) fronting your hotel, subscribe the property to them, and check that the brief reaches you every morning before service opens.

Set your waterfront alert threshold

Decide calmly what triggers action. At what status or score do you launch collection? At what point do you warn guests on arrival? A written threshold turns a stressful last-minute decision into the simple application of a rule — and aligns the whole team on the same reading.

Do this week

Set two thresholds: a "collection" threshold (you mobilize the resources) and a "communication" threshold (you inform the guest). Write them down in black and white and share them with the front-desk lead and the contractor.

Organize the collection schedule

A forecast is only worth something if it triggers an action. Line up in advance who does what: in-house team or contractor, response delay, time slots, equipment. The daily brief then becomes the input to your schedule — you mobilize the day before for the day after, instead of discovering the landing when your guests wake up.

Do before the peak

Agree with your contractor on a response delay triggered at the "collection" threshold, and share the change-of-state alert with them so they can get organized without an emergency call.

Prepare transparent guest communication

Silence worries more than candor. Prepare your messages in advance: an honest pre-stay email, a mention on the website and booking pages, a stock reply on social. Displaying conditions live — including when it's clean — builds trust and reduces disappointment on arrival. Transparency is a booking argument, not a confession.

Do before the peak

Draft your message templates for the three cases (clean / moderate / avoid) and add a "live conditions" panel to your booking page, in English, French and Spanish.

Brief the front desk

It's the front desk that fields the guest's question: "is there any sargassum?" Give them a reliable daily status and a framed answer. A team that knows how to respond, with a dated data point to back it up, turns an anxious question into a handled exchange — and protects the online review that will follow the stay.

Do before the peak

Post the daily status at the front desk and give them a short script (current state + 7-day trend + what the hotel is doing). Hold a team check-in at the start of the season.

The season calendar in the Antilles

April → October, with a May–August peak.

In the French Antilles, sargassum landings roughly span April to October, with maximum intensity between May and August. Every year differs and nothing replaces the 7-day forecast for your beach — but this rhythm sets the right tempo for preparing your steps: set-up before April, maximum vigilance in the heart of summer.
